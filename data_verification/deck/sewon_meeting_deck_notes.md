## Slide 1 — Main question: can self-evolution move beyond the initial direct-sampling frontier?

Talking points:
- Project framing: **self-evolution** — the same model searches at test time (verifier-free
  SqueezeEvolve), then we fold verified discoveries back into the SAME model (SFT now, RL next).
  No teacher anywhere.
- The three-way pass@K terminology (mandatory discipline):
  1. **current/evolving-population pass@K** — oracle of the current candidate set; tautologically
     cannot be exceeded by any selection; we never claim to.
  2. **initial direct-sampling pass@K** — the base model's first K samples; THIS is the frontier we
     claim to move.
  3. **compute-matched direct-sampling frontier, pass@(N×T)** — same total budget spent on naive
     sampling; the "is it more than sampling more?" baseline.
- One-sentence claim: *on hard problems, verifier-free evolution reaches solutions that
  compute-matched independent sampling essentially never reaches; the gains concentrate exactly at
  the difficulty boundary; folding them back into weights works so far only through selective
  (best-per-problem) SFT.*
- RSA/SE papers context (PAPER-ONLY): both measure against the evolving-population ceiling and do
  not test the initial/compute-matched frontier — that's our gap.

## Slide 2 — Frontier movement (T2 table)

- Headline (all recomputed from raw checkpoints + oracle grades, cache spot-checked by re-execution):
  of the **244 problems where 16 independent samples solve nothing**, a **second independent 16-draw
  solves 1** (and adds ZERO aggregate coverage: 850→850 of 1,093) — while SE at the SAME 32-sample
  budget solves **38**, growing to **51 at loop2** and **58 cumulative (23.8%)**.
- The two independent draws agree on 243/244 hard-zeros → the "impossible set" is a stable property
  of the sampler, not draw noise. Independent sampling saturates by ~16 samples; evolution does not.
- Honest caveats on the slide: pop-8 cross-check at matched-16 gives a modest aggregate edge
  (SE 1,384 vs BoN 1,373; +28/−17); independent-48 baseline for loop2 is MISSING (TODO);
  loop3 (partial 258-problem grading) is net −1 → evolution saturates by loop3;
  7 loop1 solves are lost at loop2 (replace-update erosion) — union numbers keep them.

## Slide 3 — Boundary buckets / curriculum signal (T3 + heatmap figure)

- Gains concentrate in buckets 1 and 2–4 (×6.2 / ×3.1 mean-correct by loop2, still climbing);
  5–8 is done after one loop; 9–15 plateaus immediately and its UNIQUE density *falls* at loop2
  (duplicates); the 0-bucket moves thinly but is the sole source of frontier expansion.
- Offline stopping-rule simulation (no live experiment yet):
  **drop only the near-saturated 9–15 bucket after loop0 → 48% of evolution compute saved with zero
  loss** (coverage 907 identical, all 58 frontier solves kept).
  **Warning result for curriculum design: a pure boundary curriculum that also drops the 0-bucket
  saves 70% but forfeits 57/58 frontier solves.** Exploration allocation on hard-zeros is load-bearing.
- Curriculum relation: per-loop improvement is a natural learnability signal (GRPO-style
  advantage-variance argument lives in the 1–8 band), but this is a hypothesis — no curriculum
  experiment has been run.

## Slide 4 — Fold-back into weights (T4 + pass@k plot)

- Status: LoRA-SFT pilots only (no RL yet). Held-out LCBv6 (zero overlap with training pool,
  verified 0/1,617 at 8-shingle containment).
- **Durable (weight-level) result:** oracle-best-per-problem code-only SFT beats base at every k
  (pass@1 0.436–0.437 vs 0.413; pass@16 0.588–0.595 vs 0.527). SE-source vs independent-source is a
  wash at matched selection (Δ0.001) — *in this small, cross-platform pilot*; SE's extra hard-tail
  coverage can't show up here by construction.
- **Selection vs durable, kept separate:** test-time selection (Phase 6) is worth up to +0.10–0.24
  pass@1 on codeforces pools; the durable SFT lift is +0.023 on LCBv6. Different quantities on
  different distributions — never mix them in one number.
- Negative results worth showing: all-unique data dumping ≤ base at pass@1 (0.397–0.410); real-CoT
  collapses to 0.20 with **78% of samples truncating at the 40,960 cap** (mechanism verified);
  final-only 0.131. The working recipe is empty-think + code-only + best-per-problem.
- Bridge to RL (Setlur et al.): SFT-on-traces is the diagnostic waypoint; boundary-set RL is the
  place a weight-level gain would be unambiguous.

## Slide 5 — Questions for Sewon

1. **Boundary curriculum**: our data says boundary buckets carry the gradient signal but the
   0-bucket carries the frontier — what's the principled split of rollout budget between
   "informative band" exploitation and hard-zero exploration? (R4-vs-R5 simulation on the slide.)
2. **Stopping rule**: is per-problem "freeze at 9-15/16 estimated density" the right test-free rule,
   given gpt-oss SVD tracks true density at r≈0.65 and never over-claims by >3 on SE pools?
3. **SFT vs RL for the fold-back**: given the SFT null between SE/independent sources at matched
   selection, is boundary-set GRPO the cleaner test of "evolution data is worth more"?
4. **Final-answer vs evolution-chain EFT**: manifests are ready (final-correct 9.1k, chain 136,
   pos/neg pairs 2k) but true parent→child lineage isn't recorded by the SE client — is state-chain
   supervision (per-loop bests) a defensible proxy, or must we re-run generation with lineage logging?
5. **Weak self-verifier vs strong judge**: the 4B cannot verify itself (SVD below random, AUC≈0.41);
   a 120B judge is ≈oracle. For a "self-evolution" paper, is judge-in-the-loop honest if disclosed,
   or do we need the single-model configuration to carry the claim?
6. **Sample-efficient RL under expensive rollouts** (Harman's framing): evolution+verification as a
   way to extract more learning signal per rollout — what evidence beyond compute-matched frontier
   movement would make this argument credible to an RL audience?
